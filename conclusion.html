Conclusion

Introduction

The lessons of the Ancient World are still extremely, but not completely, applicable to the issues of the 21st century. Augustus Caesar and Donald Trump both demonstrated the importance of the relationship between power and public perception, even after over two thousand years. Although the methods and amount of control have changed for leaders, they still heavily rely on propaganda to directly communicate with their citizens, resulting in them gaining support, maintaining influence, and strengthening their authority.

To What Extent Question

To what extent are the lessons of the Ancient World applicable to the issues of our 21st-century world?
EXTENT

To What Extent Question Analysis

NOT APPLICABLE FULLY APPLICABLE

The Ancient World's lessons on propaganda are largely, but not entirely, applicable today.

To a great extent, the lessons of Augustus's Roman era remain highly relevant to how modern politicians control power, as leaders continue to depend on carefully crafted public images to communicate directly with their supporters. While Augustus used statues, monuments, coins, and writing to depict himself as Rome's protector and a symbol of peace after the civil war, Trump used platforms such as Truth Social, X, and news platforms to communicate with his supporters and shape his and his opponents' political narratives.1 In both of these cases, the political power the leaders amassed was heavily due to their use of media, physical or digital, to sway and manipulate public perceptions. Augustus's and Trump's actions demonstrate that one's public image and authority are deeply intertwined, highlighting the relevance of lessons from the Ancient World to our 21st-century world.

However, the Ancient World's lessons are not completely applicable to the 21st-century world because of how the means of propaganda have changed and how much control modern politicians have over the media. During Augustus's rule, he faced little to no opposition because he was supported by the Roman state, allowing his image to spread without opposition.2 On the other hand, Trump operates in a digital environment where misinformation, AI, and opposing narratives constantly compete with his public image. Unlike Augustus, Trump does not have full control over how information is spread online. Although the means of propaganda and the amount of control leaders have over media have changed, the core lesson of propaganda still stands: propaganda is essential to swaying the public perception of leaders, resulting in them rising and maintaining power.

Restated Thesis

Despite both Caesar and Trump possessing varying levels of control over their media and using different means to spread their narratives, they both highlight how public and political image crafting assists leaders in obtaining and maintaining political power, as they use monuments or AI-generated content to control how others perceive them.

Final Takeaways

The pattern that leaders from different eras use for propaganda to gain and maintain power should concern citizens and societies today. During Augustus's rule, citizens saw narratives and monuments sponsored by the state on a daily basis, and they had no other alternative media sources. Augustus's power depended not only on his leadership and conquests but also on how he controlled public perception.

Although modern citizens have access to many news sources and social media platforms, propaganda remains extremely influential. Trump's use of social media, even going as far as to create his own platform, demonstrates how leaders still shape their own and opponents' narratives and communicate directly with their citizens.3 However, new environments and strategies are emerging in the modern world, including AI-generated images and videos, misinformation, and opposing narratives, which leave many citizens confused about what the actual truth is.

Because of the new digital environment, leading to various sources and narratives of the same event, media literacy will become extremely important. Similar to philosophers like Socrates and Aristotle, who proposed questions to teach citizens to think critically and question rules, modern societies will depend on citizens themselves evaluating the credibility of sources and recognizing when the media has been manipulated in a politician's favor.4 Without the skill of media literacy and questioning the credibility of sources, propaganda will allow leaders to strengthen their power without any opposition, rather than through truth alone.
